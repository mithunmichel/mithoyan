Flyght Cloud

Three modules. Multiple industries. One designer who owned it end-to-end — from the first whiteboard to enterprise client presentations that moved deals forward.

Role
Lead Product Designer
Scope
End-to-End · 3 Modules
Verticals

The Product

Flyght Cloud is a drone-agnostic, cloud-based intelligence platform — any drone, any data, any industry. The business problem was clear: drone operators were collecting enormous amounts of geospatial and video data with no scalable way to process, analyse, or act on it. I was brought in to design the product from the ground up across three distinct modules, each targeting a different market and user type.

Flyght Cloud Map
Flyght Cloud Video
Flyght Cloud Audit ⟨NDA⟩

Map Module — Turning Raw Data into GIS Intelligence

The core challenge: GIS analysts and field operators have completely different mental models, yet both needed to use the same platform. Analysts think in layers, projections, and coordinate systems. Field operators just need to upload files and get results. Designing one interface that works for both without alienating either was the central design problem.

The solution was progressive disclosure — a clean upload-to-output flow for operators, with advanced GIS controls surfaced contextually for analysts. The Map Module now supports orthomosaics, DEMs, DTMs, DSMs, and multi-layer analytics across industries from agriculture to infrastructure.

Video Module — High-Stakes Surveillance UI

ISR operators work under pressure — they need situational clarity, not visual noise. The Video Module was a product design challenge around information density: how do you surface critical intelligence from aerial footage without overwhelming the operator?

The answer was a layered display system I designed from scratch — primary feed, annotation overlays, and metadata panels that collapse and expand contextually. Operators see exactly what they need, when they need it. Nothing more.

Audit Module — Design That Moved a Deal Forward

⟨ NDA — Process artefacts shown · UI redacted ⟩ · Client: Central Government Institution, India

A large-scale government audit process was being done entirely manually — paper forms, physical site visits, manual reconciliation. The opportunity: digitise it entirely through Flyght Cloud. I owned this from brief to boardroom — user research, workflow mapping, rapid iteration, and a high-fidelity Figma prototype built and presented directly to the client institution.

The prototype wasn't just approved — it accelerated the sales process, giving the business development team a concrete, interactive vision to close with. Design as a commercial lever, not just a deliverable.

The Business Context

MapAssist isn't sold standalone — it ships with every IDEO Force military-grade and industrial UAV. That means every hardware sale is also a software experience. A bad UX here doesn't just frustrate an operator — it reflects on the entire product suite and risks the customer relationship.

The users are field operators and survey technicians running defence mapping, infrastructure inspection, and large-scale agricultural surveys. Highly skilled, mission-critical, zero tolerance for software that slows them down.

The Real Problem

The post-flight workflow was broken. Operators would land, offload storage cards, then wrestle with either developer CLIs or bloated GIS plugins to geotag their imagery before sending data to the cloud. Errors were common. Time was wasted. Two tools consistently failed operators at the same two points in their workflow.

I spent time in the field watching operators work — not just interviewing them. The pattern was consistent: the problem wasn't technical capability, it was that no existing tool was designed around how operators actually work after a flight.

The Design Decisions That Mattered

I restructured the entire flow around the operator's natural post-flight sequence — Import → Review → Geotag → Validate → Export. The map is always visible. Errors surface immediately with clear resolution paths rather than cryptic failure states. No hidden settings, no mode-switching, no cognitive overhead.

The desktop constraint became a strategic advantage — larger screen real estate meant I could show spatial data and metadata side-by-side, something impossible on mobile. Keyboard shortcuts for power users, clean defaults for everyone else. The result: geotagging errors dropped to near-zero and operators reported significantly faster post-flight processing.

PMAY Field App

How This Started

While designing the Flyght Cloud Audit module — a platform-level tool for digitising a large-scale government housing audit — I kept running into the same problem: the field collection side was entirely manual. State officials were recording data on paper. CAG field teams were doing physical verification with no digital support. The platform we were building had no way to ingest any of it.

Nobody raised this as a design problem. I raised it as a product opportunity.

Two Completely Different Users, Same Ecosystem

01
Beneficiary Upload
State government officials — non-technical, low-connectivity environments, camera-heavy data collection. Needs to be foolproof.
02
Field Verification
CAG audit teams — checklist-driven, GPS-tagged evidence capture, comparison against existing records. Needs to be precise.

Same platform ecosystem, completely different mental models, constraints, and failure modes. Designing both as one app would have been a mistake. I defined them as two separate surfaces that feed into the same backend — a product architecture decision, not just a UX one.

Why This Project Matters for My Portfolio

This project exists because I went beyond the brief. Most designers would have shipped the platform module and moved on. I identified a downstream problem that was going to undermine the entire product's value — and took initiative to define, frame, and begin designing the solution.

That's the difference between a designer who executes briefs and one who shapes products. Full case study — research synthesis, flow diagrams, wireframes, and high-fidelity prototypes — currently in development.
