Context

A gap nobody had designed for.

While working on the Flyght Cloud Audit module — a platform for digitising large-scale government housing audits — I kept running into the same structural gap. The central platform could analyse, verify remotely, and produce intelligence. But the field layer — where state officials physically inspect housing beneficiaries — was entirely manual.

State officials recorded data on paper. CAG field teams conducted physical verification with no digital support. The central platform had no reliable way to ingest any of it. Nobody raised this as a design problem. I raised it as a product opportunity.

  • Government audit workflows operate across three layers: administrative data, geospatial intelligence, and field verification
  • System-level intelligence had evolved significantly — field operations had not
  • The disconnect between what the system saw and what existed on the ground was creating unreliable audit outputs
  • No existing tool was designed around how field officers actually work under real-world constraints

The Problem

The problem isn't capturing data. It's capturing trustworthy data.

Field verification today is unstructured, inconsistent, and weakly integrated with central systems. But the deeper issue is not the volume of data collected — it's that the data cannot be verified, traced, or trusted.

Unstructured Capture
No standardised process for what to capture, in what order, or how to validate it. Each officer adapts their own approach.
"We take photos, but later it's difficult to match them to the record."
Location Inaccuracy
Device GPS often doesn't represent the actual asset location. Officers standing on roads capture coordinates that are metres away from the property.
"Sometimes we stand on the road and take photos, but the GPS is not exact."
Weak Audit Traceability
Without structured evidence and verifiable coordinates, the audit system cannot confirm field data accurately represents the asset being inspected.
"If there is doubt, we mark it and someone else has to come again."
From the field
"We reach the area, but finding the exact house takes time."

What this reveals

Field officers are not failing because they lack diligence. They're operating without tools designed for the real constraints of outdoor, time-pressured, low-connectivity fieldwork. The system expects accuracy from a process that was never set up to produce it.

The Design Reframe
❌ "How do we digitise the existing field process?"
↓
✓ "How do we design a system that produces trustworthy, auditable field evidence — even under real-world constraints?"

That shift moved the problem from form-digitisation to evidence integrity. Every subsequent decision — GPS correction, guided capture, offline-first — was a consequence of this framing.

Design Principles

Five constraints that shaped every decision.

Field officers operate outdoors, under sunlight, under time pressure, with unreliable connectivity, and with devices that produce imprecise GPS readings. Each design principle is a direct response to a real-world constraint — not a preference.

01
Designed for Real-World Conditions
Field officers work outdoors, in sunlight, on low-end devices. Aesthetic decisions had to serve legibility and usability above all else.
Decision: Light mode UI, high contrast, large touch targets. Usability over visual preference.
02
Guided Workflow over Open Capture
Unstructured input leads to unreliable data. The system must enforce consistency — not depend on each officer's judgment about what to capture.
Decision: Step-based capture flow with mandatory checkpoints. Each step confirms before proceeding.
03
Separation of Navigation and Verification
Navigation is a solved problem. The app should not try to replace Google Maps — it should focus entirely on what it does better: structured field verification.
Decision: Delegate navigation to native apps. Deep-link to Google Maps, then return to verification flow.
04
Human-Assisted Location Correction
Device GPS does not always represent the asset's location — especially when officers are standing on roads or in adjacent areas. Automation alone cannot solve this.
Decision: Map-based pin adjustment. Store original coordinates, device GPS, and corrected asset location separately — creating a transparent, auditable location model.
05
Offline-First Architecture
Connectivity is unreliable in field environments. Any step that requires internet access is a step that can silently fail — blocking the officer mid-verification with no recovery path.
Decision: All core operations — navigation, capture, form entry — work offline. Data syncs to the audit system only when connectivity is restored, non-blocking.
Platform Scalability

Designed for one scheme. Built for many.

Rather than designing a PMAY-specific tool, I architected this as a multi-scheme audit platform. The core verification workflow — location validation, evidence capture, guided submission — is scheme-agnostic. PMAY is the first active instance.

This decision was product-level thinking, not UX execution. A unified platform reduces duplication, allows the system to scale across multiple government programs, and means future schemes inherit a tested, refined workflow — not a redesigned one.

  • PMAY (Pradhan Mantri Awaas Yojana) — active, housing verification
  • PMGSY — roadmap, rural road scheme
  • Future schemes — disabled state in UI, architected for activation

Core Design Innovation

The Location Accuracy System.

The most significant design challenge in field verification is a fundamental hardware limitation: the officer's device GPS records where the officer is standing — not where the asset is located. Officers standing on a road adjacent to a property capture coordinates that may be 5–15 metres from the actual structure.

Automation cannot reliably solve this. Satellite imagery isn't always current. Map snapping introduces its own errors. The only reliable source of truth is the field officer themselves — who can see the property.

The solution: Human-assisted location correction with a fully transparent, auditable data model.

  • After capturing photo evidence, the app shows a zoomed-in satellite map
  • Three markers are displayed simultaneously: officer's device GPS, the original record pin, and the current asset pin
  • The officer drags the asset pin to the exact structure location
  • All three coordinate sets are stored independently — original, device, and verified — creating a transparent audit trail
  • No coordinates are overwritten. The system trusts human judgment while preserving the automated record for comparison
This creates a transparent and auditable location model — not a single GPS point that may be wrong, but a documented trail of where the system placed the pin, where the officer stood, and where the asset actually is.

Impact

From fragmented field ops to structured audit intelligence.

Improved location accuracy
Human-assisted pin correction addresses the fundamental GPS inaccuracy problem — the verified coordinate is the asset's location, not the officer's standing position.
Reduced repeat field visits
Guided capture and location confirmation at point of verification means ambiguous records are resolved on-site — not flagged for re-visit days later.
Standardised evidence capture
Step-based mandatory checkpoints replace ad-hoc photo collection. Every submission contains the same structured evidence set — auditable and comparable across cases.
Faster audit decision-making
Structured, verified field input enables the central audit platform to make decisions on reliable data — reducing the review overhead caused by incomplete or ambiguous submissions.
Reflection

What this project reinforced about designing for trust.

01
Real-world constraints must drive design decisions.
Every significant decision in this product — light mode, delegated navigation, offline-first, human pin correction — was a direct response to a field constraint, not a design preference. The constraints were the brief.
02
Accuracy matters more than automation in critical systems.
The pin adjustment system could have used automated GPS snapping or satellite matching. But those approaches introduce their own errors — and silently. Human correction with a transparent audit trail is more accurate and more trustworthy than automation that's wrong quietly.
03
Systems thinking is essential across platforms.
This app only makes sense as part of the audit ecosystem — field capture → structured input → audit intelligence. Designing it in isolation would have produced a data collection tool. Designing it as a system layer produced an audit input layer. The distinction matters.
04
Going beyond the brief is a product skill, not a personality trait.
Nobody asked for this. The brief was to design the Flyght Cloud audit module. I identified that the entire upstream field layer was unaddressed and would undermine the platform's value. Recognising that gap — and acting on it — is what separates product designers from screen designers.
The Bottom Line
