Context

Scale is not the challenge — visibility is.

Government audit bodies are responsible for verifying whether public funds are correctly utilised across large-scale welfare and infrastructure programs. In schemes like housing development, millions of assets are distributed across vast geographies — making physical verification slow, resource-intensive, and often incomplete.

Flyght Cloud is a geospatial intelligence platform that leverages drone-based mapping to create high-resolution digital representations of real-world environments. This project explores how such a system can transform audit from a physical process into a data-driven one.

The Problem
  • Assets are geographically dispersed — physical verification doesn't scale
  • Verification depends heavily on field visits that are slow and expensive
  • Data is fragmented across disconnected systems
  • Audit cycles are too slow; discrepancies often go undetected
  • Only a small subset of cases can ever be verified manually

The opportunity

By introducing a spatial, intelligence-driven system, auditors can verify assets remotely, identify discrepancies faster, prioritise high-risk cases, and make decisions using visual evidence.

My Role

Beyond UI — system thinking and product strategy.

I was responsible for shaping how this system would be understood, used, and trusted — from structuring an ambiguous problem into a clear product direction, all the way to presenting the solution to senior government stakeholders.

Contributions

  • Structured ambiguous problem space into clear product direction
  • Designed end-to-end workflows for auditors and analysts
  • Defined the interaction model for a map-based audit system

Key Challenges

  • No existing benchmark for a geospatial audit interface
  • Complex backend logic requiring simple user interaction
  • Multiple stakeholders with fundamentally different expectations
  • High time pressure driven by live sales requirements
  • Communicating system value clearly in early-stage client discussions

★ Breakthrough Moment
"When the interactive prototype was introduced, the dynamic shifted immediately. The client moved closer, began interacting with the interface, and started exploring the system in real time."

Context matters here: this was an early-stage sales presentation — well before any purchase order or contract. The client institution hadn't committed. The deal was live but uncertain. This was the moment that could either build conviction or lose the room.

The initial walkthrough using slides struggled to hold attention. A complex geospatial audit system explained through bullet points remained abstract — something to be evaluated in theory rather than trusted in practice.

When I introduced the interactive Figma prototype, the room changed. What was previously being explained became directly experienced. The client began navigating flows, asking "what if" questions, probing edge cases — exactly the kind of engagement that builds trust in a system before you've signed anything.

The prototype didn't close the deal — it made the deal possible. Design became the commercial accelerant: it compressed months of abstract sales conversations into a single session of direct interaction. That's what a high-fidelity prototype can do when it's built with enough fidelity to feel real.

Key UX Innovations

Simplifying complex system logic into intuitive interactions.

Each design decision was made to reduce the cognitive cost of auditing at scale. Every feature below solved a real problem identified through research — nothing was added for aesthetics alone.

Map interface
Figma frame
Map as Primary Interface
Each asset is represented spatially — replacing traditional tabular views with the geographic context that actually matters to auditors. You see where problems cluster.
Classification system
Figma frame
System-Led Classification
Assets are pre-classified as Match, Discrepancy, or Not Verifiable — reducing cognitive load by letting the system triage, so auditors focus on judgment, not sorting.
Hover / disclosure interaction
Figma frame
Progressive Disclosure
Hover-based interactions reveal key details instantly — keeping the map clean by default while making rich metadata immediately accessible when needed.
Audit workspace
Figma frame
Evidence-Centric Workspace
All relevant data — maps, drone imagery, and audit observations — are accessible in one place. No switching between tools, no lost context, no transcription errors.
Decision flow
Figma frame
Structured Decision Flow
The system guides users from initial exploration through evidence review to final audit judgment — a clear path that prevents incomplete audits and ensures consistent outcomes across the team.

Impact

The system fundamentally changes the economics of verification.

Remote
Enabled remote verification of distributed assets — auditors no longer need to travel to every site. Scale becomes tractable.
↓ Physical
Significantly reduced reliance on physical field inspections — cutting cost, time, and logistical complexity per audit cycle.
Clarity
Improved discrepancy identification through spatial comparison — patterns that were invisible in tabular data become immediately visible on the map.
↑ Speed
Accelerated audit workflows end-to-end — from data ingestion to final audit decision — compressing what previously took weeks into days.

Business Impact

  • Strengthened client confidence during early-stage discussions — the prototype gave the sales team a concrete, interactive vision to close with
  • Helped communicate a complex system effectively — design translated ambiguous backend logic into something clients could understand and trust immediately
  • Accelerated a central government deal — the interactive prototype was the pivotal moment in a high-stakes client presentation
  • Supported expansion into additional use cases — the modular architecture opened doors beyond the initial audit scope
Reflection

From interface design to system-level thinking.

This project marked a genuine transition in how I operate as a designer. The problems I was solving weren't UI problems — they were product problems. And solving them required operating well beyond the boundaries of a design brief.

What this project strengthened
01 Working with ambiguity. No benchmark exists for geospatial audit interfaces. I had to define the problem space before I could design into it.
02 Thinking at scale. Designing for millions of assets forced me to think about information architecture and progressive disclosure in ways that card-level UX doesn't require.
03 Aligning stakeholders. Government stakeholders, internal sales, product, and engineering — each with different success criteria. Holding the design vision across all of them was its own design challenge.
04 Design as a commercial lever. The prototype didn't just communicate the design — it moved a deal forward. That relationship between design quality and business outcome is now central to how I think about my work.
