Context

Scale is not the challenge — visibility is.

Central government audit bodies are responsible for verifying whether public funds are correctly utilised across large-scale welfare and infrastructure programs. In schemes like PMAY (Pradhan Mantri Awas Yojana — the Prime Minister of India's flagship housing development initiative), millions of assets are distributed across vast geographies — making physical verification slow, resource-intensive, and often incomplete.

Flyght Cloud is a geospatial intelligence platform that leverages drone-based mapping to create high-resolution digital representations of real-world environments (digital twin). This project explores how such a system can transform audit from a physical process into a data-driven one.

The Problem
  • Assets are geographically dispersed — physical verification doesn't scale
  • Verification depends heavily on field visits that are slow and expensive
  • Data is fragmented across disconnected systems
  • Audit cycles are too slow; discrepancies often go undetected
  • Only a small subset of cases can ever be verified manually

The opportunity

By introducing a spatial, intelligence-driven system, auditors can verify assets remotely, identify discrepancies faster, prioritise high-risk cases, and make decisions using visual evidence.

My Role

Beyond UI — system thinking and product strategy.

I was responsible for shaping how this system would be understood, used, and trusted — from structuring an ambiguous problem into a clear product direction, all the way to presenting the solution to senior government stakeholders.

Contributions

Key Challenges

  • No existing benchmark — this system didn't exist at this scale
  • Complex backend logic requiring simple, trustworthy user interaction
  • Multiple stakeholders with fundamentally different expectations
  • High time pressure driven by live sales requirements

★ Breakthrough Moment in a Sales Call
"When the interactive Figma prototype was introduced, the dynamic of the sales call shifted immediately. The client moved closer, began interacting with the interface, and started exploring the system in real time."

This was an early-stage sales presentation — well before any purchase order or contract. The client institution hadn't committed. The deal was live but uncertain. This was the moment that could either build conviction or lose the room.

The initial walkthrough using slides struggled to hold attention. A complex geospatial audit system explained through bullet points remained abstract — something to be evaluated in theory rather than trusted in practice.

When I introduced the interactive Figma prototype, the room changed. What was previously being explained became directly experienced. The client began navigating flows, asking "what if" questions, probing edge cases — exactly the kind of engagement that builds trust in a system before you've signed anything.

The prototype didn't close the deal — it made the deal possible. Design became the commercial accelerant: it compressed months of abstract sales conversations into a single session of direct interaction.

Critical Gap Identified During POC

The platform could verify from above.
Nobody had designed for the ground.

During the Flyght Cloud POC, a structural gap emerged that the platform itself couldn't close. The central audit system was getting smarter — but the field layer feeding it remained entirely manual, unstructured, and unreliable.

How field officers were working
Entirely manual operations
Paper-based data recording, no digital tools, no standardised process
Lat/long used inefficiently
Device GPS captured from roads and adjacent areas — coordinates didn't represent the actual asset location
No system support
Officers adapted their own processes — no structured capture, no validation, no integration with the central platform
What this created
Delays in audit cycles
Ambiguous field data required repeat visits — cases that could be closed were left open for weeks
Compounding errors
Inaccurate GPS + unstructured capture meant the central platform was making decisions on unreliable inputs
Inconsistent evidence
Every officer captured differently — photos couldn't be matched to records, evidence couldn't be compared across cases
💡 Design Opportunity

The audit intelligence platform was only as good as the data being fed into it. Fixing the platform layer without fixing the field layer meant building on an unreliable foundation.

I identified the opportunity to introduce a dedicated field verification system — a mobile-first tool that would bring structure, accuracy, and audit traceability to ground-level data collection. Not a bolt-on feature, but a purpose-built input layer for the audit ecosystem.

Reflection

From interface design to system-level thinking.

This project marked a genuine transition in how I operate as a designer. The problems I was solving weren't UI problems — they were product problems. And solving them required operating well beyond the boundaries of a design brief.

What this project strengthened
01Building something that didn't exist. There was no existing benchmark for a geospatial audit interface operating at national scale, across millions of distributed government assets. I had to define what good looked like from scratch — first of its kind.
02Working with ambiguity. No precedent, no reference point. I had to define the problem space before I could design into it — and keep the team aligned through that uncertainty.
03Thinking at scale. Designing for millions of assets forced me to think about information architecture and progressive disclosure in ways that card-level UX doesn't require.
04Design as a commercial lever. The prototype didn't just communicate the design — it moved a deal forward. That relationship between design quality and business outcome is now central to how I think about my work.
